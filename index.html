What is the AQHI?

A number that describes the air

The Air Quality Health Index (AQHI) is Canada's national air quality scale, developed by Environment and Climate Change Canada in partnership with Health Canada. It combines three-hour averages of fine particulate matter (PM2.5), ground-level ozone (O₃), and nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) into a single value reflecting short-term health risk from breathing local ambient air.

The scale

From clean to catastrophic

AQHI 1–3 is Low Risk: ideal for outdoor activity. 4–6 is Moderate Risk: at-risk groups should consider reducing exertion. 7–10 is High Risk: the general population should reschedule strenuous outdoor activity. Above 10 is Very High Risk: everyone should avoid outdoor exertion. During the 2023 wildfire season, several Canadian cities sustained AQHI readings of 10+ for days.

The consequences

What bad air does to a country

Short-term exposure can trigger asthma attacks, irritate eyes and throat, and reduce lung function. A 2025 CMAJ study documented a significant spike in asthma-related ED visits across Ontario during the June 2023 wildfire smoke episodes. Long-term PM2.5 exposure is linked to cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and an estimated 660–5,400 premature deaths annually in Canada from wildfire smoke (2019–2023).

Chapter 3 · Night

What happens
after midnight?

Traffic fades. Industry slows. The city exhales. But the air doesn't always follow.

At night, temperature inversions lock pollution at ground level. Particles linger for hours after sunset. The air you breathe in your sleep may be worse than what you breathed on your commute.

We don't notice because we're asleep — but our lungs don't rest.

Methodology & Sources

About this visualization

This project uses the Air Quality Health Index (AQHI), Canada's national air quality scale developed by Environment and Climate Change Canada in partnership with Health Canada. The AQHI is calculated from three-hour averages of ground-level ozone (O₃), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), and fine particulate matter (PM2.5), and ranges from 1 (low health risk) to 10+ (very high risk).

Important disclosure: The numerical values displayed in the visualizations are modeled to reflect documented patterns from Environment and Climate Change Canada's National Air Pollution Surveillance (NAPS) Program. All statistics quoted in the narrative text are drawn from peer-reviewed studies and official government sources, cited below.

Limitations

This is a conceptual data visualization, not a scientific publication. Numerical values in the interactive charts are generated procedurally to illustrate documented patterns, not measured directly from NAPS sensors. The narrative claims, however, are sourced from peer-reviewed and government publications as listed above. For research use, refer directly to the NAPS Data Portal.
